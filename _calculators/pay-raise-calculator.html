How to Calculate a Pay Raise

The math is straightforward. Multiply your current salary by the raise percentage expressed as a decimal, then add it to your current salary.

For example: a $60,000 salary with a 5% raise → $60,000 × 1.05 = $63,000.

But the number that actually matters is how much more hits your bank account. A $3,000 raise doesn't add $3,000 to your take-home — federal income tax and FICA both apply to that extra income first.

Why Your Raise Feels Smaller Than Expected

The US uses a progressive tax system, meaning the more you earn, the higher the rate on the top portion of your income. When you get a raise, that additional income gets taxed at your marginal rate — which is higher than the average rate on your full salary.

On top of federal tax, Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) also apply to every dollar of earned income up to the wage base. So a $5,000 raise effectively loses around 30–40% to taxes before it reaches you, depending on your bracket.

What's a Good Raise to Ask For?

In a typical year, 3–4% is considered a standard cost-of-living increase. If you're performing well and the job market is competitive, 5–8% is a reasonable ask. Promotions typically come with 10–20% bumps. If you're switching jobs entirely, a 15–25% increase is common and expected.

The single most effective thing you can do before a salary negotiation is know the market rate for your role. Sites like Glassdoor, Levels.fyi (for tech), and the Bureau of Labor Statistics Occupational Outlook Handbook all have real salary data by role and region.

Raise vs. Bonus — What's the Difference?

A raise permanently increases your base salary — it compounds with future raises and affects your benefits, 401(k) match, and any percentage-based bonuses. A one-time bonus is taxed at the supplemental rate (22% federally for most people) and doesn't raise your baseline going forward.

Over a 5-year career, a $3,000 raise is almost always worth more than a $3,000 bonus, even if the immediate dollar amount looks the same.

Frequently Asked Questions

1) How much will a 5% raise increase my paycheck?

On a $60,000 salary, a 5% raise adds $3,000 annually — about $115 extra per biweekly paycheck after federal taxes and FICA. Use the calculator above to get your exact number.

2) Is a 3% raise good?

It depends on inflation. In a normal year where inflation is around 2–3%, a 3% raise roughly maintains your purchasing power. In a high-inflation year (4–6%), a 3% raise is effectively a pay cut in real terms.

3) How do I calculate a raise percentage from two salaries?

Subtract the old salary from the new one, divide by the old salary, then multiply by 100. Example: going from $55,000 to $60,000 → ($5,000 ÷ $55,000) × 100 = 9.09%.

4) Does a raise change my tax bracket?

It might push some of your income into a higher bracket, but only the income above the bracket threshold gets taxed at the higher rate — not your entire salary. The US tax system is marginal, not a cliff.

5) How much of my raise will I actually keep?

Roughly 65–75 cents of every extra dollar, depending on your bracket and state. Federal tax (at your marginal rate) plus FICA (7.65%) accounts for the rest. The calculator above shows the exact split for your situation.
