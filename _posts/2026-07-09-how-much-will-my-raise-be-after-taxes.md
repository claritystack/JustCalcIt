<h1>How Much Will My Raise Be After Taxes?</h1>

<p>You just got a raise. Maybe it's 5%, maybe it's a flat $5,000, maybe it's a promotion bump. Either way, the number your manager told you isn't the number that hits your bank account.</p>

<p>Taxes take a cut first. And depending on your salary and where you live, that cut can be surprisingly large.</p>

<p>Here's exactly how it works — and what you'll actually keep.</p>

<h2>Short Answer</h2>

<p>For most Americans, you keep roughly <strong>65 to 75 cents of every extra dollar</strong> from a raise. The rest goes to federal income tax and FICA (Social Security and Medicare). State income tax takes even more if you're in a high-tax state like California or New York.</p>

<p>That means a $5,000 raise typically adds somewhere between $3,250 and $3,750 to your annual take-home pay — not $5,000.</p>

<h2>Why You Don't Keep the Full Amount</h2>

<p>When you get a raise, that extra income gets taxed at your <strong>marginal rate</strong> — the rate that applies to the top slice of your income, not your whole salary.</p>

<p>If you're in the 22% federal bracket, every extra dollar from your raise loses 22 cents to federal income tax right away. On top of that, Social Security takes 6.2% and Medicare takes 1.45%. That's already 29.65% gone before state taxes even enter the picture.</p>

<p>So no, a raise doesn't feel as big as it looks on paper. That's not a bug in the system — it's just how marginal taxation works.</p>

<h2>Let's Understand With an Example</h2>

<p>Here's what a $5,000 raise looks like after federal taxes and FICA across a few common salary levels, filing as single:</p>

<p><strong>$45,000 salary (12% federal bracket)</strong><br>
Federal tax on raise: ~$600<br>
FICA on raise: ~$383<br>
Extra take-home: ~<strong>$4,017 per year / $154 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<p><strong>$70,000 salary (22% federal bracket)</strong><br>
Federal tax on raise: ~$1,100<br>
FICA on raise: ~$383<br>
Extra take-home: ~<strong>$3,517 per year / $135 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<p><strong>$110,000 salary (24% federal bracket)</strong><br>
Federal tax on raise: ~$1,200<br>
FICA on raise: ~$383<br>
Extra take-home: ~<strong>$3,417 per year / $131 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<p>The higher your salary, the more of your raise goes to taxes. But you always come out ahead — a raise never makes your take-home go down.</p>

<h2>What About State Taxes?</h2>

<p>If you live in Texas, Florida, Nevada, or Washington, you pay zero state income tax on wages. Your numbers above are your real numbers.</p>

<p>In California, add roughly 9.3% state tax on that extra income at the $70k level — which drops your extra take-home closer to $2,800 on a $5,000 raise. New York takes a similar bite.</p>

<p>State taxes can make a meaningful difference, especially for larger raises. That's why two people with identical salaries in different states can end up with noticeably different take-home pay.</p>

<h2>Does a Raise Push You Into a Higher Tax Bracket?</h2>

<p>It might — but it doesn't work the way most people fear.</p>

<p>The US tax system is <strong>marginal</strong>, which means only the income above a bracket threshold gets taxed at the higher rate. Not your entire salary.</p>

<p>If your raise pushes $2,000 of income from the 22% bracket into the 24% bracket, only those $2,000 get taxed at 24%. The rest of your raise still gets taxed at 22%. Your total paycheck doesn't shrink — it just grows a bit less than it might have otherwise.</p>

<p>The myth that a raise can "cost you money" by bumping your bracket is exactly that — a myth.</p>

<p><strong>Step 2:</strong> Multiply your raise amount by that rate to get federal tax on the raise.</p>
<p><strong>Step 3:</strong> Multiply your raise by 7.65% for FICA (6.2% SS + 1.45% Medicare).</p>
<p><strong>Step 4:</strong> Add your state marginal rate if applicable.</p>
<p><strong>Step 5:</strong> Subtract all of that from your raise amount. What's left is your actual take-home increase.</p>

<h2>Raise vs Bonus: Which Puts More in Your Pocket?</h2>

<p>This comes up a lot. A $5,000 raise and a $5,000 bonus are not the same thing financially — even before taxes.</p>

<p>A raise permanently increases your base salary. That compounds with future raises, affects your 401(k) match if it's percentage-based, and raises your baseline for any future negotiations. A bonus is a one-time payment that doesn't change your salary going forward.</p>

<p>Tax-wise, bonuses are often withheld at the 22% supplemental federal rate regardless of your actual bracket, which can mean over-withholding if you're in a lower bracket. A raise is withheld at your normal rate spread across paychecks.</p>

<p>Over a 5-year career, a $3,000 raise almost always outperforms a $3,000 bonus in total financial impact — even if the immediate dollar amount looks identical.</p>
