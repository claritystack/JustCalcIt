<h1>$30 an Hour Is How Much a Year?</h1>

<p>$30 an hour sounds solid. But what does it actually look like stretched across a full year — after taxes, after bills, in real purchasing power?</p>

<p>That's what this breaks down. Not just the math, but what $30/hr actually means for your life.</p>

<h2>The Basic Math</h2>

<p>Working 40 hours a week, 52 weeks a year:</p>

<p><strong>$30 × 40 × 52 = $62,400 per year</strong></p>

<p>That's your gross income — before taxes touch it. If you take two weeks unpaid vacation, it drops to $60,000 (50 weeks × 40 hours × $30).</p>

<p>Most people work somewhere in between, so think of <strong>$60,000–$62,400</strong> as your realistic gross salary range at $30/hr.</p>

<h2>$30 an Hour Broken Down Every Which Way</h2>

<p><strong>Annual:</strong> $62,400<br>
<strong>Monthly:</strong> $5,200<br>
<strong>Biweekly paycheck:</strong> $2,400<br>
<strong>Weekly:</strong> $1,200<br>
<strong>Daily (8hrs):</strong> $240</p>

<h2>What You Actually Take Home After Taxes</h2>

<p>At $62,400, you're solidly in the <strong>22% federal bracket</strong> as a single filer. Here's what the deductions look like:</p>

<p><strong>Single filer, no state income tax (TX, FL, NV):</strong><br>
Federal income tax: ~$6,618<br>
Social Security (6.2%): ~$3,869<br>
Medicare (1.45%): ~$905<br>
<strong>Take-home: ~$51,008/yr → ~$1,962 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<p><strong>Single filer, California:</strong><br>
Add roughly $2,800 in state income tax on top<br>
<strong>Take-home: ~$48,200/yr → ~$1,854 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<p><strong>Married filing jointly, no state tax:</strong><br>
Federal income tax drops significantly due to wider brackets and higher standard deduction<br>
<strong>Take-home: ~$53,500/yr → ~$2,058 per biweekly paycheck</strong></p>

<h2>Is $30 an Hour a Good Wage?</h2>

<p>Depends entirely on where you live — and that answer varies more than most people expect.</p>

<p>At $62,400 a year, you're earning <strong>above the US median individual income</strong>, which sits around $56,000–$58,000. By that measure, $30/hr puts you in the upper half of American earners.</p>

<p>But "above median" means very different things in different cities:</p>

<p><strong>Where $30/hr is comfortable:</strong> Most of the Midwest, the South, rural areas generally. In cities like Columbus, Memphis, Kansas City, or San Antonio, $62,400 is a genuinely good income. You can rent a decent apartment, own a car, save money, and not be stressed about groceries.</p>

<p><strong>Where $30/hr is tight:</strong> San Francisco, New York City, Seattle, Boston, Los Angeles. In these markets, $62,400 after taxes leaves you around $4,200/month — and a one-bedroom apartment alone can run $2,500–$3,500+. It's livable, but there's not much left over.</p>

<p><strong>Where $30/hr is genuinely solid:</strong> Mid-sized cities like Austin, Denver, Nashville, Charlotte, or Phoenix. You're not rich, but you're stable. Rent is manageable, you can save, and you're not living paycheck to paycheck.</p>

<h2>What Jobs Pay Around $30 an Hour?</h2>

<p>$30/hr sits at an interesting crossroads — above entry-level, below high-skill professional. Jobs commonly in this range include:</p>

<p>Electricians, plumbers, and HVAC technicians (trades often land $25–$40/hr depending on experience and location). Registered nurses in lower cost-of-living states. Mid-level web developers or IT support roles. Administrative managers and executive assistants at larger companies. Dental hygienists. Physical therapy assistants.</p>

<p>It's also the target range for many people moving out of minimum wage or early-career jobs — $30/hr often represents the first point where finances start to feel manageable rather than constantly strained.</p>

<h2>How Does $30/hr Compare to a Salary?</h2>

<p>If a company offers you a $62,400 salary versus a $30/hr hourly role, they're technically equivalent — but they're not identical in practice.</p>

<p>Salaried workers typically don't get overtime pay. Hourly workers at $30/hr who work 45 hours a week earn $30 × 1.5 for those extra 5 hours — an extra $75/week, $3,900/year. Over time, that adds up to a meaningful difference if overtime is common in your role.</p>

<p>On the flip side, salaried positions more often include benefits, more job security, and easier access to promotions. Neither is universally better — it depends on the role and the employer.</p>

<h2>How to Get From $20/hr to $30/hr</h2>

<p>If you're currently earning around <a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/blog/20-an-hour-is-how-much-a-year/" target="_blank">$20 an hour</a>, getting to $30 is a 50% pay increase — significant, but not unrealistic within a few years depending on your field.</p>

<p>The most direct paths: moving into a skilled trade or getting a certification that commands higher pay, switching employers (job-hopping consistently outperforms loyalty for salary growth), or moving into a supervisory or specialist role within your current field. A <a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/pay-raise-calculator/" target="_blank">raise of that size</a> rarely happens through annual reviews alone — it usually requires a deliberate career move.</p>
